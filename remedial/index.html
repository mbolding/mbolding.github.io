Overall Strategy

This plan is designed for a 12-18 month timeline with part-time study alongside work. The curriculum covers the core physics PhD coursework typically completed in the first 1-2 years of graduate school. You can adjust the pace based on your schedule—consistency is key. Even 5-10 hours per week will allow you to work through this material effectively.

Curriculum Overview

Phase 1: Mathematical Foundations

2-3 Months

Build the mathematical toolkit that supports all other physics courses. This foundation is essential and will be used constantly throughout your studies.

  • Complex analysis (contour integration, residue theorem, analytic continuation)
  • Advanced differential equations (PDEs, boundary value problems, Green's functions)
  • Group theory basics (symmetries, representation theory)
  • Tensor analysis and differential geometry fundamentals

Phase 2: Classical Mechanics

2-3 Months

Develop deep intuition for Lagrangian and Hamiltonian methods that you'll apply in quantum mechanics and other advanced topics.

  • Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalism in depth
  • Canonical transformations and generating functions
  • Hamilton-Jacobi theory
  • Small oscillations and normal modes
  • Introduction to chaos and nonlinear dynamics
  • Continuum mechanics and field theory formulation

Phase 3: Electromagnetism

3-4 Months

Directly relevant to MRI! This is worth spending extra time on given its direct application to your work.

  • Electrostatics with boundary value problems and Green's functions
  • Magnetostatics and vector potential formulation
  • Time-varying fields and Maxwell's equations in full generality
  • Electromagnetic waves in various media (conductors, dielectrics, plasmas)
  • Waveguides and resonant cavities (very MRI-relevant!)
  • Radiation theory (dipole, multipole)
  • Special relativity from E&M perspective
  • Covariant formulation of electromagnetism

Phase 4: Quantum Mechanics

3-4 Months

Essential foundation for understanding nuclear spin physics and magnetic resonance phenomena at a fundamental level.

  • Formal structure (Hilbert spaces, operators, Dirac notation)
  • Angular momentum and spin theory
  • Time-independent and time-dependent perturbation theory
  • Variational methods
  • WKB approximation
  • Scattering theory
  • Identical particles and quantum statistics
  • Approximation methods for multi-electron atoms

Phase 5: Statistical Mechanics

2-3 Months

Foundation for understanding thermal physics, many-body systems, and relaxation phenomena in magnetic resonance.

  • Ensemble theory (microcanonical, canonical, grand canonical)
  • Partition functions and thermodynamic quantities
  • Quantum statistical mechanics
  • Phase transitions and critical phenomena
  • Fluctuations and response theory
  • Introduction to non-equilibrium statistical mechanics

Phase 6: Advanced Topics (Optional)

2-3 Months

Select topics based on your interests and relevance to your MRI work.

  • Condensed matter physics (magnetic resonance is fundamentally a condensed matter phenomenon)
  • Introduction to quantum field theory
  • Advanced topics in magnetic resonance theory

Detailed Course Syllabi

Mathematical Methods for Physicists

Recommended Textbooks

  • Primary: Boas, Mathematical Methods in the Physical Sciences (gentler start)
  • Advanced: Arfken, Weber & Harris, Mathematical Methods for Physicists

Course Outline (10 weeks)

Weeks 1-2: Complex Analysis

Review complex analysis fundamentals, then dive into contour integration, residue theorem, branch cuts, and analytic continuation. Work lots of problems—this is a crucial computational tool.

Weeks 3-4: Partial Differential Equations

Separation of variables, Sturm-Liouville theory, orthogonal functions. Green's functions for ODEs and PDEs. Practice with heat equation, wave equation, and Laplace equation.

Weeks 5-6: Special Functions

Study Bessel, Legendre, spherical harmonics, Hermite, and Laguerre functions. These appear everywhere in physics problems with specific symmetries.

Weeks 7-8: Transform Methods

Fourier analysis (series and transforms), Laplace transforms. For MRI work, really master Fourier transforms!

Weeks 9-10: Group Theory and Tensors

Introduction to group theory—symmetry operations, group representations, applications to physics. Tensors and basics of differential geometry.

Key Skills to Develop: Recognize which mathematical technique applies to which physical problem. Build computational facility through extensive problem-solving.

Classical Mechanics

Recommended Textbooks

  • Primary: Goldstein, Poole & Safko, Classical Mechanics (the standard)
  • Alternative: Taylor, Classical Mechanics (more pedagogical)
  • Advanced supplement: Landau & Lifshitz, Mechanics

Course Outline (12 weeks)

Weeks 1-2: Lagrangian Formalism

Review of Newtonian mechanics, then develop Lagrangian formalism from D'Alembert's principle. Calculus of variations. Constraints and Lagrange multipliers.

Weeks 3-4: Hamiltonian Mechanics

Legendre transformations, Hamilton's equations, phase space. Poisson brackets and canonical transformations.

Weeks 5-6: Hamilton-Jacobi Theory

Hamilton-Jacobi theory and action-angle variables. This builds intuition for the quantum-classical correspondence.

Week 7: Oscillations

Small oscillations, normal modes, and coupled oscillators. This has direct applications in NMR/MRI (coupled spin systems).

Week 8: Rigid Body Dynamics

Euler angles, inertia tensor, Euler's equations. Gyroscopes and precession (think magnetic moment precession!).

Weeks 9-10: Continuum Mechanics

Transition from discrete to continuous systems. String waves, membranes, and field formulations.

Weeks 11-12: Advanced Topics

Introduction to chaos and nonlinear dynamics. Perturbation theory. Special topics based on interest.

Key Skills to Develop: Fluency in moving between Lagrangian, Hamiltonian, and Newtonian formulations. Understanding symmetries and conservation laws.

Electromagnetism

Recommended Textbooks

  • Primary: Griffiths, Introduction to Electrodynamics (bridge from undergrad)
  • Advanced: Jackson, Classical Electrodynamics (the graduate standard—notoriously difficult but comprehensive)
  • Alternative: Zangwill, Modern Electrodynamics (newer, excellent pedagogy)

Course Outline (14 weeks)

Weeks 1-2: Electrostatics

Coulomb's law, Gauss's law, electric potential. Laplace and Poisson equations. Method of images.

Week 3: Boundary Value Problems

Solving Laplace's equation with separation of variables in Cartesian, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates. Multipole expansion.

Week 4: Green's Functions

Green's functions for electrostatics. This is powerful for solving complex geometries.

Weeks 5-6: Magnetostatics

Biot-Savart law, Ampere's law, vector potential. Magnetic fields in matter, magnetization. Very relevant for understanding MRI field distributions!

Week 7: Time-Varying Fields

Faraday's law, displacement current, Maxwell's equations in full glory. Electromagnetic energy and momentum.

Weeks 8-9: Electromagnetic Waves

Wave equation, plane waves, polarization. Waves in conductors and dielectrics. Dispersion and absorption. Reflection and refraction.

Week 10: Waveguides and Cavities

Rectangular and cylindrical waveguides. Resonant cavities and quality factor. This is directly applicable to MRI RF coils and cavities!

Weeks 11-12: Radiation Theory

Electric dipole radiation, magnetic dipole, higher multipoles. Radiation from accelerating charges. Antenna theory basics.

Weeks 13-14: Relativistic Electromagnetism

Special relativity and covariant formulation of electromagnetism. Four-vectors, tensors, Lorentz transformations. Field tensor.

Key Skills to Develop: Mastery of solving boundary value problems. Deep understanding of waves and resonance. Facility with vector calculus.

Quantum Mechanics

Recommended Textbooks

  • Primary: Griffiths, Introduction to Quantum Mechanics (excellent pedagogical bridge)
  • Advanced: Sakurai & Napolitano, Modern Quantum Mechanics (graduate standard)
  • Alternative: Shankar, Principles of Quantum Mechanics (very mathematical)
  • Supplement: Cohen-Tannoudji, Quantum Mechanics (encyclopedic)

Course Outline (14 weeks)

Weeks 1-2: Formal Foundations

Hilbert spaces, operators, eigenvalues. Dirac notation. Measurement postulates. Uncertainty principle rigorously derived.

Week 3: Time Evolution

Schrödinger and Heisenberg pictures. Time-evolution operator. Ehrenfest's theorem connecting classical and quantum.

Weeks 4-5: Angular Momentum

Orbital and spin angular momentum. Ladder operators. Addition of angular momenta. Clebsch-Gordan coefficients. Spin-1/2 systems (fundamental for NMR!).

Week 6: Hydrogen Atom

Hydrogen atom revisited with full angular momentum theory. Fine structure, hyperfine structure (relevant for understanding different nuclei in MRI).

Weeks 7-8: Time-Independent Perturbation Theory

Non-degenerate and degenerate cases. Applications to atoms in external fields (Zeeman effect—directly relevant to how MRI works!).

Week 9: Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory

Fermi's golden rule. Transition rates. This explains RF excitation in MRI.

Week 10: Approximation Methods

Variational methods and WKB approximation. Practical tools for approximating solutions.

Weeks 11-12: Scattering Theory

Born approximation, partial wave analysis, phase shifts. While less directly relevant to MRI, it's part of the standard curriculum.

Weeks 13-14: Identical Particles

Fermions and bosons, Pauli exclusion principle. Multi-electron atoms. Density matrix formalism (useful for understanding relaxation in NMR).

Key Skills to Develop: Comfort with operator formalism and Dirac notation. Understanding perturbation theory. Ability to solve real quantum systems.

Statistical Mechanics

Recommended Textbooks

  • Primary: Pathria & Beale, Statistical Mechanics
  • Alternative: Kardar, Statistical Physics of Particles
  • Concise: Chandler, Introduction to Modern Statistical Mechanics

Course Outline (11 weeks)

Weeks 1-2: Foundations

Microstates and macrostates, ergodic hypothesis, entropy. Microcanonical ensemble. Connection to thermodynamics.

Weeks 3-4: Canonical Ensemble

Partition function, Helmholtz free energy, applications to ideal gases, paramagnetism (relevant for understanding MRI signal source!), harmonic oscillators.

Week 5: Grand Canonical Ensemble

Chemical potential and applications to quantum gases.

Weeks 6-7: Quantum Statistical Mechanics

Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein statistics. Applications to electrons in metals, photon gas, phonons.

Weeks 8-9: Phase Transitions

Mean field theory, Ising model, critical phenomena, universality. Landau theory.

Week 10: Fluctuations and Response

Fluctuation-dissipation theorem. Connection to relaxation processes (T1, T2 in MRI!).

Week 11: Non-Equilibrium Statistical Mechanics

Introduction to non-equilibrium processes. Master equation. Boltzmann equation basics.

Key Skills to Develop: Understanding connection between microscopic and macroscopic. Partition function calculations. Understanding thermal equilibrium and relaxation.

Study Approach Recommendations

For Each Subject

  1. Read the chapter/section carefully, working through derivations on paper yourself
  2. Solve problems—lots of them. Aim for 5-10 problems per topic. Graduate physics is learned by doing problems
  3. For MRI-relevant topics (especially E&M), try to connect back to your daily work
  4. Keep a running document of key results, formulas, and insights
  5. Consider working through problems from actual PhD qualifying exams (many universities post these online)

Integration with Your MRI Work

Watch for these particularly relevant connections:

  • Classical E&M: RF coils, B₀ field design, gradient coils
  • Quantum mechanics: Nuclear spin physics, Bloch equations, relaxation
  • Statistical mechanics: Thermal equilibrium magnetization, Boltzmann distribution of spin states
  • Math methods: k-space formalism is just Fourier analysis

Resources Beyond Textbooks

  • MIT OpenCourseWare has excellent physics graduate courses with lecture notes and problem sets
  • Stanford, Berkeley, and other universities often post qualifying exam problems
  • Physics Forums and Physics Stack Exchange for when you get stuck
  • Consider finding a study partner or online study group

Reality Check: This is a lot of material. Don't feel like you need to master everything to the level of a physics PhD student taking qualifying exams. Focus on building solid understanding and problem-solving skills. Some topics you'll breeze through given your background; others will require more time.

The electromagnetism and quantum mechanics sections are probably most valuable for your MRI work, so if you need to prioritize, weight those more heavily.
